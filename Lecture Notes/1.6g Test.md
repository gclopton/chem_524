# 1.6g Potentials in Electrochemical Thermodynamics (February 18, 2026)



Electrochemical thermodynamics tracks two coupled driving forces. Chemical transformations and mass transfer are governed by changes in Gibbs free energy, while charged species additionally experience electrical work in an electrostatic field. A consistent framework therefore requires (i) a definition of the electrical potential associated with a phase, (ii) a clear statement that experimentally meaningful potentials are differences between phases, and (iii) a free-energy measure that combines chemical and electrical contributions for ions and electrons. This chapter segment introduces the ==inner potential== (==Galvani potential==), the ==electrode potential==, the ==chemical potential==, and the ==electrochemical potential==.

> [!definition] Galvani potential (inner potential)
> The ==inner potential== (or ==Galvani potential==) of a phase is defined operationally through work on a test charge. Consider bringing a positive test charge from a point at infinity to a point in the interior of a phase, along a quasi-static path. The electrical work required per unit charge defines the potential of that interior point relative to infinity. [This should not be wrapped in a definition callout. If you verbalized the equation shown below in a proper way, then you shouldn't need this definition here anyway.]



> [!equation] Galvani potential (test-charge definition)
> $$
> \phi=\frac{w}{q}.
> $$

This equation says that $\phi$ is the reversible electrical work per unit positive charge required to bring a test charge from infinity into the phase interior. It therefore has units of energy per charge, i.e., volts:
$$
1~\mathrm{V}=1~\mathrm{J/C}.
$$
The inner potential is thus a property of the electrical environment of the phase, including interfacial charge distributions that establish a potential field . The definition emphasizes that $\phi$ pertains to the interior of a phase and is introduced by the idealized “transport a unit positive charge from infinity” thought experiment . 



> [!figure] Figure 1.6g.1.1: Test-charge definition of the Galvani potential $\phi^\alpha$
> ![[Lecture Notes/images/Lecture 2026-02-18 Figure 1 Galvani potential definition.png|500]]
> The sketch shows a phase labeled $\alpha$ with an interior point labeled “Pt.” A positive test charge is brought from “$\infty$” along a path into the phase interior. The reversible work $w$ required to bring a test charge $q>0$ into the interior defines the Galvani (inner) potential at that point through $\phi=w/q$. The diagnostic point is that $\phi^\alpha$ is defined for the *interior* of a phase, even though interfacial charge distributions can contribute to the electric field that sets the interior potential.




## 1.6g.2 Electrode potential ($E$) as a potential difference between phases

> [!definition] electrode potential
> An ==electrode potential== is fundamentally a potential difference between phases associated with an interfacial arrangement (most commonly a metal phase in contact with an electrolyte phase). Because absolute electrical potentials are not directly observable in isolation, electrochemical measurements are constructed from differences: between phases across an interface, or between an electrode of interest and a chosen reference electrode.

Consequently, the theoretical development is organized to express potentials in difference form and to connect those differences to measurable cell voltages.


## 1.6g.3 Chemical potential ($\mu_i$) as partial molar Gibbs free energy



> [!definition] chemical potential
> The ==chemical potential== $\mu_i$ is the partial molar Gibbs free energy of species $i$, defined by the derivative of $G$ with respect to $n_i$ at fixed $T$, $P$, and all other amounts $n_{j\neq i}$.

The chemical driving force for species $i$ is quantified by the ==chemical potential==, defined as the partial derivative of Gibbs free energy with respect to the amount of that species at fixed temperature, pressure, and the amounts of all other species:

> [!equation] Chemical potential (definition)
> $$
> \mu_i=\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial n_i}\right)_{T,P,n_{j\neq i}}.
> $$

This equation says that $\mu_i$ is the change in Gibbs free energy per mole of $i$ added under the stated constraints, i.e., a partial molar Gibbs free energy.

This definition shows that $\mu_i$ is the change in Gibbs free energy per mole of $i$ added under the stated constraints, i.e., a partial molar Gibbs free energy. For solutions and mixtures, the dependence of $\mu_i$ on composition is commonly expressed through activity:

> [!equation] Chemical potential from activity
> $$
> \mu_i^\alpha=\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln a_i^\alpha.
> $$

This equation says that composition (and non-ideality) enters the chemical driving force through the logarithm of the dimensionless activity $a_i^\alpha$ of species $i$ in phase $\alpha$, referenced to a standard chemical potential $\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}$. This logarithmic form is the principal mechanism by which composition enters thermodynamic driving forces in electrochemical systems.

## 1.6g.4 Electrochemical potential ($\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha$): chemical plus electrical contributions

> [!definition] electrochemical potential
> The ==electrochemical potential== $\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha$ is the molar free energy of species $i$ in phase $\alpha$ including both chemical and electrical contributions.

For a charged species, chemical free energy alone does not determine equilibrium or transport. If species $i$ has charge number $z_i$ and resides in phase $\alpha$ with inner potential $\phi^\alpha$, then placing one mole of that species into the phase requires (or releases) electrical work proportional to $z_iF\phi^\alpha$, where $F$ is Faraday’s constant. The combined quantity is the ==electrochemical potential==:

> [!equation] Electrochemical potential (definition)
> $$
> \bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
> \qquad
> z_i=\text{charge number}.
> $$

This equation says that electrochemical potential is the molar free energy of a species including chemical and electrical work contributions. It provides the thermodynamic “potential” that drives diffusion and migration of ions and governs electrochemical equilibrium across phase boundaries.

Starting with the activity representation of the chemical potential in phase $\alpha$,
$$
\mu_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big)
$$
and the definition of electrochemical potential,
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
$$
substitution yields
$$
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}
&= \mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha} \\
&= \left(\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big)\right)+z_iF\phi^{\alpha} \\
&= \mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big).
\end{aligned}
$$
Define a standard electrochemical potential for phase $\alpha$ by grouping the reference-like terms,
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}\equiv\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}.
$$
Then the electrochemical potential takes the compact standard-plus-activity form
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big).
$$
This expression makes explicit that the electrochemical potential differs from the chemical potential by an electrical contribution tied to the Galvani potential of the phase, while retaining the familiar $RT\ln(\text{activity})$ structure.

> [!equation] Electrochemical potential (activity form)
> $$
> \bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big)+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}.
> $$

This equation says that electrochemical potential has the same “standard + $RT\ln a$” structure as chemical potential, with an added electrical term $z_iF\phi^\alpha$. It is the form that is substituted into equilibrium statements to obtain Nernst-type relations.


> [!Lessons] Lessons Learned: Activity-form electrochemical potential
> **Lesson 1:** The activity form of $\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha$ is obtained by direct substitution of electro chemical potential in activity form $\mu_i^\alpha=\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln a_i^\alpha$ into the definition of electrochemical potential $\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha=\mu_i^\alpha+z_iF\phi^\alpha$.
> 
> **Lesson 2:** Grouping the standard-state terms into $\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}$ keeps the familiar “standard + $RT\ln a$” structure while making the electrical contribution $z_iF\phi^\alpha$ explicit.




## 1.6g.5 Working rules and equilibrium conditions

The definitions of chemical potential and electrochemical potential immediately yield a set of practical rules that simplify common cases and state equilibrium conditions succinctly.




### 5.1 Pure phases (e.g., $\mathrm{Ag(s)}$, $\mathrm{Zn(s)}$)

> [!exercise] Exercise 2: Simplifying $\bar{\mu}_i$ for a pure condensed phase
> For a pure condensed phase, the activity of the component in its standard state is defined as $a_i=1$. For a neutral species, $z_i=0$.
> 
> Using the definitions
> $$
> \bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i+z_iF\phi,
> \qquad
> \mu_i=\mu_i^{\circ}+RT\ln(a_i),
> $$
> simplify $\bar{\mu}_i$ for this case.


For a pure condensed phase, the activity of the component in its standard state is defined as unity, $a_i=1$. For a neutral species, $z_i=0$. Under these conditions, the electrochemical potential reduces to the standard chemical potential.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\mu}_i
&= \mu_i+z_iF\phi \\
&= \left(\mu_i^{\circ}+RT\ln(a_i)\right)+z_iF\phi \\
&= \mu_i^{\circ}+RT\ln(1)+0\cdot F\phi \\
&= \mu_i^{\circ}.
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!Lessons] Lessons Learned: Pure-phase simplifications
> **Lesson 1:** When a species is in its pure condensed standard state, $a_i=1$ so the activity term collapses because $\ln(1)=0$.
> 
> **Lesson 2:** When a species is neutral ($z_i=0$), the electrical contribution vanishes, so electrochemical and chemical potentials coincide for that species in that phase.

$$
\begin{aligned}

$$

> **Lesson 1:** The electron’s charge number is negative ($z_e=-1$), so its electrochemical potential decreases with increasing Galvani potential: $\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{M}}=\mu_e^{\mathrm{M}}-F\phi^{\mathrm{M}}$.

### 5.3 Species equilibrium between two phases


> [!questions] Title
> 1. state the condition of equilibrium across phases
> 2. state the reaction equilibrium condition


A species $i$ is at equilibrium with respect to transfer between two different phases $\alpha$ and $\beta$ when its electrochemical potential is equal in both phases:

> [!equation] Equilibrium across phases (electrochemical potential)
> $$
> \bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\bar{\mu}_i^{\beta}.
> $$

This equation says that transfer equilibrium between phases requires equality of electrochemical potential, so both activity (chemical) and Galvani-potential (electrical) contributions are included for ions.

### 5.4 Reaction equilibrium within a single phase

For a reaction occurring entirely within a single phase $\alpha$, equilibrium requires the balance of electrochemical potentials between reactants and products:

$$
\sum \bar{\mu}^{\alpha}_{\text{reactants}}=\sum \bar{\mu}^{\alpha}_{\text{products}}.
$$

Equivalently, using stoichiometric coefficients $\nu_i$ (positive for products, negative for reactants),

> [!equation] Reaction equilibrium condition (electrochemical potentials)
> $$
> \sum_i \nu_i\,\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=0.
> $$

This equation says that reaction equilibrium can be written as a stoichiometric sum of electrochemical potentials equal to zero, which is especially convenient for half-reactions and interfacial equilibria involving charged species.

> The measurable electrical quantity is the Galvani-potential difference between the copper terminals.
>

> 
> **Part B:** Starting from electrochemical-potential equilibrium statements and expansions, derive the Nernst-form expression for $E$ in terms of activities for the net reaction $\mathrm{Zn}(s)+2\mathrm{H}^+(S)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+\mathrm{H_2}(g)$.

To relate an electrode potential to thermodynamic state variables, it is useful to embed the electrode of interest in a complete cell whose terminal potentials are defined at metallic leads. Consider the composite cell written with two distinct copper leads,  
$$
\mathrm{Cu}\,|\,\mathrm{Zn}\,|\,\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)\,;\,;\,\mathrm{H}^+(S)\,|\,\mathrm{H_2}(g)\,|\,\mathrm{Pt}\,|\,\mathrm{Cu}'.
$$
where $\mathrm{Cu}$ and $\mathrm{Cu}'$ denote two different copper terminals (the copper leads of the connectors). The measurable electrical quantity is taken as the Galvani-potential difference between these terminals,

> [!equation] Terminal potential difference between metal leads
> $$
> E\equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}.
> $$

This equation says that the measured cell voltage is a Galvani-potential difference between two metal terminals (leads), i.e., the voltage a voltmeter reads across the terminals.

#### 6.1 Left-side reduction written against electrons in the copper lead

Three equilibrium statements are combined to rewrite the zinc half-cell in a form that explicitly uses electrons in the external metal lead.

First, electrons are at equilibrium between the copper lead and the zinc metal,  
$$
\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}.
$$
Second, the interfacial redox equilibrium at zinc is  
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}+2e^- &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}, \\
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}+2\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}} &= \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Third, $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}$ is at equilibrium between the zinc interface and the bulk solution (with $S$ denoting solution),  
$$
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}.
$$

Adding these relations eliminates the intermediate $\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}$ and $\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}$, yielding an effective left-half-cell equilibrium written in terms of $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}$ in solution and electrons in the copper terminal:

$$
\boxed{2\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}}
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+2e^-(\mathrm{Cu})\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{Zn}).
$$

Expanding the electrochemical potentials using $\bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i+z_iF\phi$, with $z_e=-1$, $z_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}=+2$, and $z_{\mathrm{Zn}}=0$, gives the explicit “left half-cell” equation (labeled as Eq. 2 in the notes):

$$
\boxed{
2\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}\big)+2F\phi^{S}
=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}
}
\tag{Eq. 2}
$$

#### 6.2 Right-side reduction (hydrogen) written against electrons in the copper lead

On the right side, the hydrogen equilibrium is written with electrons in the $\mathrm{Cu}'$ terminal:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2\mathrm{H}^+(S)+2e^-(\mathrm{Cu}') &\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H_2}(g), \\
2\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S}+2\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}'} &= \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Expanding each electrochemical potential in the same way (with $z_{\mathrm{H}^+}=+1$, $z_e=-1$, $z_{\mathrm{H_2}}=0$) and using $\mu=\mu^\circ+RT\ln a$ produces the right-half-cell equation (labeled as Eq. 1):

$$
\boxed{
2\Big(\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}+RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})+F\phi^{S}\Big)
+2\Big(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}\Big)
=\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}+RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g})
}
\tag{Eq. 1}
$$

#### 6.3 Forming the terminal potential difference and identifying the Nernst form

The electron chemical-potential term that appears in Eq. 2 is not directly accessible by itself. The standard elimination step is to combine the two half-cell relations so that the electronic terms cancel. Subtracting Eq. 1 from Eq. 2 and using metallic equilibrium of electrons between the copper terminals,  
$$
\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}'}.
$$
removes the unknown electronic contribution and isolates the terminal Galvani-potential difference.





$$
\begin{aligned}
&\Big(2\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}+RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S})+2F\phi^{S}\Big) \\
&\qquad-\Big(2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}+2RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})+2F\phi^{S}+2\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-2F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}\Big) \\
&\qquad=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}-\Big(\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}+RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g})\Big) \\
\Longrightarrow\;& 2\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-2F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+2F\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}+RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S})-2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}-2RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S}) \\
&\qquad=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}-\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}-RT\ln(a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}) \\
\Longrightarrow\;& 2F(\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}) \\
&\qquad=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}+2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}-\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}
-RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}}{(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})^2}\right) \\
\Longrightarrow\;& 2F(\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}) \\
&\qquad=-\Big(\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}+\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}-2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}\Big)
-RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}}{(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})^2}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

The standard-state combination is identified as the standard Gibbs free energy of reaction for  
$$
\mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{s})+2\mathrm{H}^+(S)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+\mathrm{H_2}(g),
$$
namely  
$$
\Delta G_r^\circ=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\circ,S}+\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{\circ,g}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\circ,\mathrm{Zn}}-2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{\circ,S}.
$$
With $E\equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$, the result becomes
$$
2F E=-\Delta G_r^\circ-RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}}{(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})^2}\right).
$$
Using $(\Delta G_r^\circ=-nF E^\circ)$ with $(n=2)$ gives the Nernst-form expression for the terminal potential difference:

$$E=E^{\circ}-\frac{R T}{2 F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g}{\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right)^2}\right)$$

This equation says that the measurable terminal voltage differs from the standard potential $E^\circ$ by a composition-dependent term involving the reaction quotient for the net reaction $\mathrm{Zn}(s)+2\mathrm{H}^+(S)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+\mathrm{H_2}(g)$, with the expected prefactor $RT/(nF)$ for $n=2$ electrons.

> [!Lessons] Lessons Learned: Eliminating unmeasurable electron terms
> **Lesson 1:** Write half-cell equilibria in electrochemical-potential form, then combine them so that electron terms cancel; the remaining $\phi$ terms assemble into a measurable terminal potential difference.
> 
> **Lesson 2:** After cancellation, the activity dependence must match the net reaction quotient; checking exponents and signs is a quick diagnostic for bookkeeping errors.

## 1.6g.7 Ion transfer at an ITIES: a Nernst-type relation from electrochemical potentials


> [!questions] Title
> 1. Define the following terms:
> a. ITIES
> b. ion-transfer reaction



> [!definition] interface between two immiscible electrolyte solutions (ITIES)
> An ==interface between two immiscible electrolyte solutions (ITIES)== is a boundary between two liquid electrolyte phases (for example, water and nitrobenzene) across which ions can transfer while the liquids remain macroscopically immiscible.

> [!definition] ion-transfer reaction
> An ==ion-transfer reaction== is an interfacial process in which an ionic species moves from one liquid phase to the other across an ITIES without undergoing electron-transfer (redox) chemistry.

An interface between two immiscible electrolyte solutions (ITIES) supports ion-transfer reactions in which an ionic species moves from one liquid phase to the other without undergoing electron-transfer (redox) chemistry. The thermodynamic description is obtained by enforcing equality of electrochemical potentials of the transferring ion across the two phases. This produces a Nernst-type relation between the ==interfacial Galvani potential difference== and the ion activities in each phase.

#### 7.1 Overall electrochemical reaction and Faradaic character

For acetylcholine as a monovalent cation, the overall transfer reaction from water (W) to nitrobenzene (NB) is written, including phases, as  
$$
\mathrm{ACh}^+(W)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{ACh}^+(NB)
$$

This process is **non-redox**: no electrons appear, and no oxidation states change. Nevertheless, it is **Faradaic** in the electrochemical sense that it involves **net transfer of charge across the interface** carried by an ionic species (as opposed to purely capacitive charging of the interfacial double layers). The corresponding current is proportional to the rate of ion transfer across the interface.




> [!figure] Figure 1.6g.7.1: Ion transfer of $\mathrm{ACh}^+$ across an ITIES
> ![[Lecture Notes/images/Lecture 2026-02-18 Figure 2 ITIES ion transfer sketch.png|500]]
> The sketch shows two immiscible liquid phases separated by an interface: nitrobenzene ($NB$, top) and water ($W$, bottom). The ion-transfer process is depicted by arrows crossing the interface, indicating motion of acetylcholine between phases. The diagnostic point is that the species identity is the same ion on each side (here $\mathrm{ACh}^+$), so the “reaction” is transfer between phases rather than an electron-transfer half-reaction.



#### 7.2 Electrochemical-potential correlation and first-step expansion



At equilibrium with respect to transfer, the electrochemical potential of $\mathrm{ACh}^+$ must be the same in both phases:
$$
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}.
$$

The electrochemical potential in a phase $\alpha\in\{W,NB\}$ is expanded (first-step expansion) in terms of standard chemical potential, activity, and inner (Galvani) potential:
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
=\mu_i^{\circ,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big)+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
$$

For $\mathrm{ACh}^+$, $z=+1$. Substituting the expansion into the equilibrium condition gives the explicit “Nernst starting point”:

$$
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}\big)+F\phi^{W}
=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}\big)+F\phi^{NB}
$$

#### 7.3 Derivation Mode: interfacial potential difference (Nernst form for ion transfer)

The interfacial Galvani potential difference (organic relative to water) is

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B} \equiv \phi^{N B}-\phi^W .
$$

Then:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}\big)+F\phi^{W}
&=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}\big)+F\phi^{NB} \\
F(\phi^{NB}-\phi^{W})
&=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}
+RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}}\right) \\
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}
&=
\frac{\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}}{F}
+\frac{RT}{F}\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

The standard Gibbs free energy of transfer for $W \rightarrow N B$ is defined by

$$
\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\mathrm{o}, W \rightarrow N B} \equiv \mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^{\mathrm{o}, N B}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^{\mathrm{o}, W} .
$$



It is common to express the standard-state term using a standard Gibbs free energy of transfer. Define
$$
\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\circ,W\to NB}\equiv \mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}.
$$
Then $\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{\circ,NB}=-\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\circ,W\to NB}$, and the result becomes


$$\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=-\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\mathrm{o}, W \rightarrow N B}}{F}+\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^W}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^{N B}}\right)$$



This equation says that the interfacial potential difference required for transfer equilibrium depends on a standard transfer free energy (reflecting solvation preference between phases) and on the logarithm of the activity ratio across the interface, with the same $RT/(zF)$ voltage scaling that appears in the usual Nernst equation.

> [!Lessons] Lessons Learned: ITIES Nernst-type relations
> **Lesson 1:** For ion transfer across an ITIES, the equilibrium condition is equality of electrochemical potentials of the ion in the two phases; no electrons appear in the reaction itself.
> 
> **Lesson 2:** Expanding $\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha$ in activity form produces a standard-state offset plus a logarithmic activity-ratio term; rearranging isolates the interfacial Galvani potential difference $\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}$.
> 
> **Lesson 3:** Packaging the standard-state offset as $\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\circ,W\to NB}$ makes the physical meaning explicit: it encodes the ion’s relative solvation preference between the two liquids.
