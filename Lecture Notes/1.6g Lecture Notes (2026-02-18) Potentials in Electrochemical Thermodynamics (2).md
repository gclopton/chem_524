

Electrochemical thermodynamics tracks two coupled driving forces. Chemical transformations and mass transfer are governed by changes in Gibbs free energy, while charged species additionally experience electrical work in an electrostatic field. A consistent framework therefore requires (i) a definition of the electrical potential associated with a phase, (ii) a clear statement that experimentally meaningful potentials are differences between phases, and (iii) a free-energy measure that combines chemical and electrical contributions for ions and electrons. This chapter segment introduces the inner (Galvani) potential, the electrode potential, the chemical potential, and the electrochemical potential.



# 1. Inner potential (Galvani potential) ($\phi$)

Electrochemical systems cannot be described using chemical free energy alone, because the species that move and react at electrodes are charged. Any thermodynamic accounting that predicts equilibrium conditions, driving forces for transport, or measurable voltages must therefore include electrical work in addition to chemical work. To do that cleanly, the framework needs an operational way to assign an electrical potential to the interior of each phase (metal, electrolyte, membrane, etc.) so that electrical contributions can be written down and later combined with chemical potentials for ions and electrons. So this leads us to define the inner potential:

> [!equation] inner potential
> 
> $$
> \phi=\frac{w}{q}.
> $$

The inner potential ($\phi$) of a material phase is defined as the total thermodynamic work ($w$) performed by an external agent to move an infinitesimal test charge ($q$) from a vacuum at infinity into the bulk of that phase, normalized by the charge's magnitude. To ensure the phase's charge distribution and polarization remain undisturbed, the test charge is assumed to be sufficiently small and is moved along a quasi-static path. This approach ensures that the process is reversible—eliminating energy losses from friction or radiation—and accounts for the cumulative work against all electrical forces encountered across the phase boundary.

In practice, this definition provides the formal coordinate system necessary for electrochemical thermodynamics. While the absolute inner potential of a single phase is not directly measurable-since inserting any physical probe would create a new interface and an additional, unknown potential drop-defining $\phi$ allows us to rigorously partition the energy of a system. By assigning a potential $\phi^\alpha$ to each phase, we can systematically express interfacial voltage drops as differences ( $\phi^\beta$ - $\phi^\alpha$ ) and combine electrical work terms ( $z_i F \phi^\alpha$ ) with chemical potentials ( $\mu_i$ ). This creates the electrochemical potential ( $\bar{\mu}_i$ ), a unified driving force that governs the equilibrium and transport of charged species across the boundaries we will now examine.


> [!definition] Electrode Potential
> The difference in inner potential ($\phi$) between a conducting electrode phase (typically a metal or semiconductor) and the adjacent electrolyte phase with which it is in contact.


> [!figure] figure 1
> ![[Lecture Notes/images/Lecture 2026-02-18 Figure 1 Galvani potential definition.png|500]]
> An infinitesimal positive test charge ($\oplus$) is transported from a reference point at infinity ($\infty$) into the bulk of phase $\alpha$ along a quasi-static path. The total thermodynamic work required to reach the interior point ($Pt$), normalized by the magnitude of the test charge, defines the absolute inner potential $\phi_\alpha$ relative to the vacuum at infinity.





# 2. Chemical and Electrical Potential

In any electrochemical system, species don't just move because of electric fields; they move because they "want" to be in a state of lower chemical energy. Whether it is lithium ions intercalating into a graphite electrode or oxygen molecules adsorbing onto a catalyst surface, there is an inherent energetic preference for certain environments. To predict if a reaction will occur spontaneously or in which direction an ion will diffuse, we need a single variable that captures this "chemical pressure." This is the role of the chemical potential—it serves as the fundamental link between the macroscopic laws of thermodynamics and the microscopic behavior of individual species.

The chemical driving force for species $i$ is quantified by the chemical potential, defined as the partial derivative of Gibbs free energy with respect to the amount of that species at fixed temperature, pressure, and the amounts of all other species:


> [!equation] Chemical potential
> $$
> \mu_i=\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial n_i}\right)_{T,P,n_{j\neq i}}.
> $$

Much like height in a gravitational field or voltage in an electric circuit, the chemical potential tells you the "altitude" of a species' energy. Species will naturally flow from regions of high $\mu_i$ to low $\mu_i$. It quantifies how much the system's total energy changes when a single particle is added; effectively, it is the 'altitude' of a species on an energetic landscape. Just as fluids flow down a pressure gradient, molecules spontaneously move from regions of high chemical potential to low chemical potential.


Practically, the chemical potential serves as the "chemical half" of the total driving force that moves ions and electrons through a cell. In electrochemical methods, we rarely deal with "pure" chemistry or "pure" electricity; instead, we observe their coupled effect. For instance, in a battery or fuel cell, a species might be pulled toward an electrode by an electric field but pushed away by a concentration gradient. By quantifying the chemical potential, we can determine the open-circuit voltage of a cell—the point where the chemical "push" exactly balances the electrical "pull." It allows us to predict how changing the concentration of an electrolyte or the composition of an alloy will shift the measured potential ($E$), providing the theoretical basis for techniques like potentiometry and the Nernstian behavior of electrodes. Without this term, we would have no way to account for the energy stored in chemical bonds or the entropy of mixing, both of which are as vital to a battery's operation as the wires connecting it.


## 2.1 Defining Chemical Potential in terms of Chemical Activity


While the definition of $\mu_i$ tells us what the chemical driving force is, it doesn't tell us how to calculate it for a real-world substance like a salt in an electrolyte or a lithium atom in an oxide. To make the chemical potential useful for engineering calculations, we need to separate the intrinsic energy of the species from the statistical effects of its concentration. This allows us to define a "home base" for the energy and then account for how "crowded" or "active" the species is in its current environment.

> [!equation] Chemical potential (activity form)
> $$
> \mu_i=\mu_i^{0}+RT\ln(\alpha_i).
> $$

where $\mu_i^{0}$ is the standard chemical potential and $\alpha_i$ is the activity. In physical terms, this equation states that the total chemical potential of a species is the sum of its baseline energy ($\mu_i^0$) and a thermal-stochastic correction ($RT \ln a_i$). The Standard Chemical Potential ($\mu_i^0$) represents the "fixed altitude" of the species in a specific reference state (the standard state). It captures the internal bonding energy and the inherent "nature" of the molecule or ion. The Activity ($a_i$) represents the "effective concentration." It accounts for how much of the species is available to react. The $RT \ln(a_i)$ term represents the entropic "push" that comes from the thermal energy of the system ($RT$) and the probability of finding the species in a given volume ($\ln a_i$). As a species becomes more concentrated or "active," its chemical potential—its urge to escape or react—increases logarithmically.

This decomposition is the mathematical engine behind the Nernst Equation. In the lab, we don't measure $\mu_i^0$ directly; we measure how the electrode potential ($E$) shifts as we change the concentration of the reactants. Because the electrical work must balance this chemical "push," every ten-fold change in activity (one "decade") results in a predictable millivolt shift in the measured voltage. Practically, this allows us to use electrochemical cells as sensors: by measuring the voltage, we are effectively measuring the activity ($a_i$) of a specific ion in solution. This is the fundamental principle behind pH meters, ion-selective electrodes, and the monitoring of state-of-charge in battery management systems.


## 2.2 Electrochemical Potential

In isolation, neither the inner potential nor the chemical potential provides the full picture of an ion’s behavior. An ion in an electrolyte doesn't care if its energy comes from a concentration gradient or an electric field—it simply responds to the total energetic landscape. To describe the "complete" driving force in electrochemical systems, we must combine these two terms. This summation allows us to treat charged species with the same thermodynamic rigor as neutral ones, creating a single "master" potential that dictates where ions will move and when a reaction will stop. For a charged species, chemical free energy alone does not determine equilibrium or transport. If species $i$ has charge number $z_i$ and resides in phase $\alpha$ with inner potential $\phi^\alpha$, then placing one mole of that species into the phase requires (or releases) electrical work proportional to $z_iF\phi^\alpha$, where $F$ is Faraday’s constant. The combined quantity is the electrochemical potential:



> [!equation] Electrochemical potential
> 
> $$
> \bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
> \qquad
> z_i=\text{charge number}.
> $$
> 


The electrochemical potential ($\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}$) is the total work required to move one mole of a charged species $i$ from a vacuum at infinity into the bulk of phase $\alpha$. It is the sum of two distinct "energy costs": (1) the Chemical Term ($\mu_i^{\alpha}$), which represents the energy associated with the chemical environment, including bonding, short-range interactions, and the entropic "push" of concentration, and (2) The Electrical Term ($z_i F \phi^{\alpha}$), which represents the energy required to move the species' charge ($z_i F$) into the electrical potential ($\phi^{\alpha}$) of that phase. If the chemical potential is the "altitude" of the species, the electrical term is like a "lift" or "weight" added to that altitude. Together, they define the true energy state of the ion.



$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
\qquad
z_i=\text{charge number}.
$$

The electrochemical potential is the foundational variable for all experimental measurements, as it defines the "finish line" for any electrochemical process. In the lab, when we measure an **Open Circuit Voltage (OCV)**, we are observing the state where the electrochemical potential of a species is identical across the interface ($\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha} = \bar{\mu}_i^{\beta}$); at this point, the electrical potential difference exactly cancels out the chemical "push," and net current ceases. When we use a potentiostat to apply an overpotential, we are intentionally unbalancing these electrochemical potentials to create a "slope" that forces ions to migrate or electrons to transfer. Practically, this means that every "voltage" we read on an instrument is actually a reflection of the system's attempt to equalize these total energy levels. Whether you are performing Cyclic Voltammetry to study reaction kinetics or Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy to probe transport, you are ultimately measuring how the system's $\bar{\mu}_i$ responds to external perturbations.



### 2.1.1 Grouping Electrochemical Terms

To make the electrochemical potential as mathematically workable as the standard chemical potential, we often look for a way to map its structure onto the familiar equations of neutral thermodynamics. While $\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha$ explicitly separates the "chemical" and "electrical" halves of the energy, we can group the terms that represent the fixed, environment-specific baseline of the phase. This allows us to treat a charged ion with the same logarithmic activity dependence we use for neutral molecules, effectively absorbing the phase's electrical "floor" into a single reference value.

Starting with the activity representation of the chemical potential in phase $\alpha$,
$$
\mu_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{0}+RT\ln\!\big(\alpha_i^{\alpha}\big)
$$
and the definition of electrochemical potential,
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
$$
substitution yields
$$
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}
&= \mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha} \\
&= \left(\mu_i^{0}+RT\ln\!\big(\alpha_i^{\alpha}\big)\right)+z_iF\phi^{\alpha} \\
&= \mu_i^{0}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(\alpha_i^{\alpha}\big).
\end{aligned}
$$
Define a standard electrochemical potential for phase $\alpha$ by grouping the reference-like terms,
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}\equiv\mu_i^{0}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}.
$$
Then the electrochemical potential takes the compact standard-plus-activity form
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(\alpha_i^{\alpha}\big).
$$

This expression reveals that the electrochemical potential follows the exact same "thermal-stochastic" scaling as a neutral species, but with a shifted baseline. Physically, it means that while the inner potential ($\phi^\alpha$) of the phase changes the "starting altitude" of the ion's energy, the way that concentration (activity) adds to that energy remains identical to the laws of pure chemistry. By defining $\bar{\mu}_i^{0,\alpha}$, we are essentially establishing a phase-specific "home base" that accounts for both the intrinsic bonding energy of the species and the electrical environment of the phase it inhabits.



### 2.1.2 Working rules and equilibrium conditions

The definitions of chemical potential and electrochemical potential immediately yield a set of practical rules that simplify common cases and state equilibrium conditions succinctly.

#### 5.1 Pure phases (e.g., $\mathrm{Ag(s)}$, $\mathrm{Zn(s)}$)

For a pure condensed phase, the activity of the component in its standard state is defined as unity, $a_i=1$. For a neutral species, $z_i=0$. Under these conditions, the electrochemical potential reduces to the standard chemical potential.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\mu}_i
&= \mu_i+z_iF\phi \\
&= \left(\mu_i^{0}+RT\ln(a_i)\right)+z_iF\phi \\
&= \mu_i^{0}+RT\ln(1)+0\cdot F\phi \\
&= \mu_i^{0}.
\end{aligned}
$$

##### When to use this condition


If the species is a pure condensed phase-typical examples are metals like $\mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{s}), \operatorname{Ag}(\mathrm{s})$, solids like $\mathrm{AgCl}(\mathrm{s}), \mathrm{PbSO}_4(\mathrm{~s})$, or a pure liquid component serving as a phase-then its activity is defined as 1 in its standard state. For a neutral pure species, the electrochemical potential reduces to a constant standard chemical potential:

$$
\bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i^0
$$


This is used constantly in electrode problems because metal electrodes and many solid salts are pure phases. It is not something to "assume"; it is the thermodynamic convention for pure condensed phases. If the problem explicitly says "pure solid," "solid electrode," or shows $(s)$ with no mixture, you should expect to set $a=1$.

Two cautions that help on exams: first, this does not apply to ions in solution (even if the solution is mostly water); second, it does not apply to gases (unless a specific standard-state activity is chosen for gases, which is a different convention, not "pure condensed phase").


#### 5.2 Electrons in a metal

For an electron, $z_e=-1$. In a metal phase (for example Zn), the electrochemical potential is therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}
&= \mu_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}+z_eF\phi^{\mathrm{Zn}} \\
&= \mu_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}-F\phi^{\mathrm{Zn}}.
\end{aligned}
$$


##### When to use this condition

Electrons are special because they are charged and live in a metal phase. Every time you see $\bar{\mu}_e^\alpha$ with $\alpha$ a metal phase, you should automatically write

$$
\bar{\mu}_e^\alpha=\mu_e^\alpha-F \phi^\alpha
$$

since $z_e=-1$.

This is the workhorse in the "complete cell with copper leads" derivation, because you need to express electron terms in the same coordinate system before they can cancel. On an exam, if the goal is a measurable terminal voltage and the problem involves metal leads or junctions, you should expect to write at least one electron equilibrium condition and then expand $\bar{\mu}_e$ using this rule.


#### 5.3 Species equilibrium between two phases

A species $i$ is at equilibrium with respect to transfer between two different phases $\alpha$ and $\beta$ when its electrochemical potential is equal in both phases:

$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\bar{\mu}_i^{\beta}.
$$


##### When to use this condition

This is your interface rule. If a species $i$ is present on both sides of an interface (or can be exchanged across it) and the problem says that interface is at electrochemical equilibrium, then you may write

$$
\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha=\bar{\mu}_i^\beta .
$$


This is exactly what you do for ITIES ion transfer:

$$
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^W=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^{+}}^{N B},
$$

and it is also what you do for "electrons are at equilibrium across the $\mathrm{Cu} \mid \mathrm{Zn}$ junction" or "electrons are at equilibrium across $\mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime \prime \prime}$ except there $i=e$ and the phases are metals.

How do you know which species get a 5.3 condition at a given interface? The interface itself tells you. At a metal|metal junction, the transferable species you care about is the electron. At a metal|solution boundary, the transferable species might be an ion (if you're explicitly treating partition) but more commonly you're using a reaction equilibrium (5.4) for the redox process at that boundary. At a liquid|liquid ITIES boundary, the transferable species is the ion that crosses (e.g., $\mathrm{ACh}^{+}$).

A quick test: if the species appears as the "same chemical identity" in both phases (same $i$, different superscript), and the problem context is transfer/partition, then 5.3 is the right statement.


#### 5.4 Reaction equilibrium within a single phase

For a reaction occurring entirely within a single phase $\alpha$, equilibrium requires the balance of electrochemical potentials between reactants and products:

$$
\sum \bar{\mu}^{\alpha}_{\text{reactants}}=\sum \bar{\mu}^{\alpha}_{\text{products}}.
$$

Equivalently, using stoichiometric coefficients $\nu_i$ (positive for products, negative for reactants),

$$
\sum_i \nu_i\,\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=0.
$$



##### When to use this condition


This is the chemistry rule. When a reaction is asserted to be at equilibrium "at the electrode" or "at the interface," you write the stoichiometric balance of electrochemical potentials for that reaction: $\sum_i \nu_i \bar{\mu}_i^\alpha=0$ (all species evaluated in the same phase $\alpha$ where the reaction is taking place).

This is what you wrote for zinc deposition/dissolution and for the hydrogen electrode. It's not about transfer of one species across a boundary; it's about reactants/products interconverting at equilibrium.

How to decide between 5.3 and 5.4 in practice:
If the statement is "species $i$ is the same thing on both sides of a boundary" (partition/transfer), use 5.3. If the statement is "these reactants turn into these products" (chemical transformation, usually redox at an interface), use 5.4.



#### Strategy for knowing when to use what


When you sit down with an exam problem, do this in the same order every time.

First, list the phases in the cell diagram (metals, solutions, gases, organic phase). Anything separated by a vertical bar is an interface.

Second, mark which interfaces are stated to be in equilibrium. If the prompt says "all interfaces at electrochemical equilibrium," that's a green light to write all the needed 5.3 and 5.4 relations.

Third, at each interface decide whether you need a transfer condition (5.3) or a reaction condition (5.4). Metal|metal junction: 5.3 for electrons. ITIES: 5.3 for the transferring ion. Electrode reaction site: 5.4 for the redox reaction, written in the phase where you are evaluating each species' $\bar{\mu}$ per your class convention.

Fourth, expand each $\bar{\mu}$ using $\bar{\mu}=\mu^0+R T \ln a+z F \phi$. Apply 5.1 immediately for pure solids ( $a=1$, often $z=0$ ). Apply 5.2 immediately whenever $e^{-}$appears.

Fifth, only after everything is expressed in comparable coordinates do you subtract/collect terms to eliminate $\mu_e$ (identical leads) and isolate the measurable potential difference.



## 2.3 Eliminating the electron term in a measurable cell potential


> [!exercise] Deriving the Nernst Equation for a Complete Cell
> Consider a galvanic cell composed of a zinc electrode and a standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) connected by identical copper leads:
> 
> $$
> \mathrm{Cu}|\mathrm{Zn}| \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S) ; ; \mathrm{H}^{+}(S)\left|\mathrm{H}_2(g)\right| \mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}
> $$
> 
> 
> Assuming all interfaces are at electrochemical equilibrium, derive the expression for the measurable terminal potential difference $E=\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$ in terms of the activities of the participating species and the standard cell potential $E^{\circ}$.



To relate an electrode potential to thermodynamic state variables, it is useful to embed the electrode of interest in a complete cell whose terminal potentials are defined at metallic leads. Consider the composite cell written with two distinct copper leads,  
$$
\mathrm{Cu}\,|\,\mathrm{Zn}\,|\,\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)\,;\,;\,\mathrm{H}^+(S)\,|\,\mathrm{H_2}(g)\,|\,\mathrm{Pt}\,|\,\mathrm{Cu}'.
$$
where $\mathrm{Cu}$ and $\mathrm{Cu}'$ denote two different copper terminals (the copper leads of the connectors). The measurable electrical quantity is taken as the Galvani-potential difference between these terminals,
$$
E\equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}.
$$



#### 6.1 Left-side reduction written against electrons in the copper lead

Right now, we have a mismatch of coordinates. We want to measure the electrical state of the system at the copper lead ( Cu ), but the chemical reaction ( $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}+ 2 e^{-} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}$ ) is physically happening inside the zinc metal ( Zn ). While the zinc is physically in the electrode, we want to ignore its internal potential and treat the whole electrode assembly as a single functional unit.

At this stage, the zinc half-cell is "hidden" behind two internal interfaces: the Copper-Zinc junction and the Zinc-Solution boundary. If we try to write a single equation for the redox reaction now, it will be "stuck" using the energy of electrons inside the zinc, which we can't measure with our external copper wires. To solve this algebraically, we need to perform a series of substitutions-treating the internal zinc phase as an intermediate node that we can mathematically "cancel out." Our goal is to derive an effective equilibrium expression that skips the zinc entirely, linking the ions in the solution directly to the electrons in our copper lead.


Three equilibrium statements are combined to rewrite the zinc half-cell in a form that explicitly uses electrons in the external metal lead.

First, electrons are at equilibrium across the copper-zinc junction:

$$
\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}} . \tag{1}
$$


Second, the interfacial redox equilibrium at zinc is:

$$
\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}+2 e^{-} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}, \quad \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} \tag{2}
$$


Third, $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}$ is at equilibrium between the zinc-side interfacial region and the bulk solution $S$ :

$$
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} \tag{3}
$$


Substituting (1) and (3) into (2) eliminates the intermediate zinc electron and zinc-side ionic electrochemical potentials:

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn} 2^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} & \\
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} & \left(\text { substitute } \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right) \\
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} & \left(\text { substitute } \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)
\end{array}
$$


Thus the effective left-half-cell equilibrium, written directly in terms of $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}$ in solution and electrons in the copper terminal, is

$$
2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+2 e^{-}(\mathrm{Cu}) \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{Zn}) .
$$


Now expand electrochemical potentials using $\bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i+z_i F \phi$, with $z_e=-1, z_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}=+2$, and $z_{\mathrm{Zn}}=0$

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} \\
2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}+z_e F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)+\left(\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S+2 F \phi^S\right) & =\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}+0 \cdot F \phi^{\mathrm{Zn}} \\
2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S+2 F \phi^S & =\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} \\
2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S+2 F \phi^S & =\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}} .
\end{aligned}
$$


Finally, write the solution chemical potential in activity form and take the pure solid zinc reference (activity $\approx 1$ ) as a constant:

$$
\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right), \quad \mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{\mathrm{Zn}}=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}
$$


Substituting gives the left half-cell equation:

$$
\boxed{2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right)+2 F \phi^S=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}} \tag{Eq. 2}
$$


By substituting the equilibrium conditions of the internal junctions, we have moved our reference point for the electrons from the zinc interior to the copper terminal. We now have a direct link between the electrical potential of the wire ($\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$) and the chemical state of the electrolyte ($a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}$), without needing to know the absolute potential inside the Zinc itself.



#### 6.2 Right-side reduction (hydrogen) written against electrons in the copper lead

We now apply the same logic to the right-hand side of the cell. Just as we "collapsed" the zinc electrode, we must now anchor the hydrogen reaction-which occurs on the Platinum surface -to the second copper terminal ( $\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ ). The physical reality is that the $2 \mathrm{H}^{+}+2 e^{-} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_2$ reaction utilizes electrons from the platinum phase, but our voltmeter only sees the electrons in the attached copper lead. By treating the platinum phase as another intermediate node and assuming electrochemical equilibrium at the $\mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ junction, we can rewrite the hydrogen half-cell reaction as if it were occurring directly against the electrons in our second copper terminal. This ensures that both halves of our cell are defined within the same "coordinate system".

Start from the interfacial redox equilibrium written in the platinum phase (the physical electron reservoir at the reaction site):

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \mathrm{H}^{+}(S)+2 e^{-}(\mathrm{Pt}) & \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_2(g), \\
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g .
\end{aligned}
$$


Impose electron equilibrium across the $\mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ junction:

$$
\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}
$$


Substitute this into the redox condition to rewrite it against electrons in the copper terminal:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g \\
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g .
\end{aligned}
$$


Now expand $\bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i+z_i F \phi$ with $z_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}=+1, z_e=-1, z_{\mathrm{H}_2}=0$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g \\
2\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+F \phi^S\right)+2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}\right) & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g+0 \cdot F \phi^g \\
2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S+2 F \phi^S+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g
\end{aligned}
$$


Finally, write chemical potentials in activity form:

$$
\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S=\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right), \quad \mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g=\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right),
$$

and substitute:

$$
\boxed{2\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right)+F \phi^S\right)+2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}\right)=\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right)} \tag{Eq. 1}
$$


We now have two symmetrical equations (Eq. 1 and Eq. 2) that describe the left and right terminals in identical terms. Each equation is still "burdened" by an unmeasurable chemical potential of electrons ($\mu_e$), but because both terminals are made of the same metal (copper), these unknown terms are now perfectly positioned for elimination. We have successfully linked the chemical activities of both half-cells to the electrical potentials of our measurement leads.

#### 6.3 Forming the terminal potential difference and identifying the Nernst form

At this point, we have two symmetrical equations—one for each terminal—but they are both still plagued by the unmeasurable electron chemical potential ($\mu_e$). Because this term is an intrinsic property of the metal itself, we cannot isolate it using electrical measurements alone. However, we can exploit a clever mechanical symmetry: since both terminals are made of identical copper, their electronic chemistry is exactly the same. By subtracting the two half-cell equations, we can mathematically cancel out these inaccessible electronic terms. This final subtraction allows us to isolate the potential difference ($E$) and link it directly to the total change in chemical free energy for the full cell reaction.


To eliminate the unmeasurable electron chemical potential, substitute the explicit terminal forms (Eq. 2 for the zinc side and Eq. 1 for the hydrogen side) and take the difference (Eq. 2) - (Eq. 1). This subtraction is chosen because both relations are written against copper leads; since the two terminals are the same metal, the electron chemical potentials are identical and cancel: $\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}$. Carrying out the subtraction also removes the common solution potential term $2 F \phi^S$, leaving only the measurable terminal difference $E \equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$.



$$\begin{aligned} & \left(2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right)+2 F \phi^S\right) \\ & -\left(2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right)+2 F \phi^S+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right) \\ & =\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}-\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right)\right) \\ \Longleftrightarrow & 2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}-2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right)-2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right) \\ & =\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}-\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}-R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right) \\ \Longleftrightarrow & -2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}-\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}+2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}-R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right)-R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right)+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right) \\ & \quad\left(\text { use } \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right) \\ \Longleftrightarrow & 2 F\left(\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}+2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}-\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}-R T\left(\ln \left(a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S\right)+\ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g\right)-2 \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right)\right) \\ \Longleftrightarrow & 2 F\left(\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0, \mathrm{Zn}}+2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0, S}-\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}-R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^S a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^g}{\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^S\right)^2}\right) .\end{aligned}$$

By using identical copper leads, we have completely scrubbed the equation of all internal, unmeasurable variables—specifically the inner potentials of the zinc and platinum, and the chemical potential of the electrons. What remains on the left is the purely electrical terminal voltage, and what remains on the right is a collection of purely chemical terms. Now, we must translate this into a standard thermodynamic form. The grouping of $\mu^0$ terms represents the energy change of the system when it moves from a state of pure reactants to pure products under standard conditions. By identifying this grouping as the Standard Gibbs Free Energy of Reaction ($\Delta G_r^\circ$), we can transform this into the familiar Nernst format.


The standard-state combination is identified as the standard Gibbs free energy of reaction for  
$$
\mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{s})+2\mathrm{H}^+(S)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Zn}^{2+}(S)+\mathrm{H_2}(g),
$$
namely  
$$
\Delta G_r^\circ=\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{0,S}+\mu_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{0,g}-\mu_{\mathrm{Zn}}^{0,\mathrm{Zn}}-2\mu_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{0,S}.
$$
With $E\equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}'}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$, the result becomes
$$
2F E=-\Delta G_r^\circ-RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}}{(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})^2}\right).
$$
Using $(\Delta G_r^\circ=-nF E^\circ)$ with $(n=2)$ gives the Nernst-form expression for the terminal potential difference:
$$
\boxed{
E=E^\circ-\frac{RT}{2F}\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S}a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g}}{(a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S})^2}\right)
}
$$
which is the desired measurable potential in terms of activities and a standard potential.




We have now connected the "visible" voltage read by a voltmeter to the "invisible" chemical driving forces of the internal reaction. By ensuring our measurement terminals were of the same material, we eliminated all internal complexities—the specific potential of the Zinc, the Platinum, and the electrons themselves—leaving only the fundamental relationship between electrical work and chemical activity. We have proven that the electrode potential is not just an arbitrary value, but a direct measurement of the system's thermodynamic urge to reach equilibrium.





> [!definition] ITIES (Interface between Two Immiscible Electrolyte Solutions)
> A phase boundary formed when two electrolyte solutions that do not mix—typically an aqueous phase (water) and an organic phase (such as nitrobenzene or 1,2-dichloroethane)—are brought into contact.



> [!definition] Ion transfer (IT)
> The primary mechanism of charge transport in an ion transfer reaction where an ion $i$ moves across the boundary, defined by the equilibrium $\bar{\mu}_i^W = \bar{\mu}_i^{org}$.

### 7. Ion transfer at an ITIES: a Nernst-type relation from electrochemical potentials

An interface between two immiscible electrolyte solutions (ITIES) supports **ion-transfer reactions** in which an ionic species moves from one liquid phase to the other without undergoing electron-transfer (redox) chemistry. The thermodynamic description is obtained by enforcing equality of **electrochemical potentials** of the transferring ion across the two phases. This produces a Nernst-type relation between the **interfacial Galvani potential difference** and the ion activities in each phase.

#### 7.1 Overall electrochemical reaction and Faradaic character

For acetylcholine as a monovalent cation, the overall transfer reaction from water (W) to nitrobenzene (NB) is written, including phases, as  
$$
\mathrm{ACh}^+(W)\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{ACh}^+(NB)
$$

This process is **non-redox**: no electrons appear, and no oxidation states change. Nevertheless, it is **Faradaic** in the electrochemical sense that it involves **net transfer of charge across the interface** carried by an ionic species (as opposed to purely capacitive charging of the interfacial double layers). The corresponding current is proportional to the rate of ion transfer across the interface.

> ![[Lecture Notes/images/Lecture 2026-02-18 Figure 2 ITIES ion transfer sketch.png|500]]



#### 7.2 Electrochemical-potential correlation and first-step expansion

At equilibrium with respect to transfer, the electrochemical potential of $\mathrm{ACh}^+$ must be the same in both phases:
$$
\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}.
$$

The electrochemical potential in a phase $\alpha\in\{W,NB\}$ is expanded (first-step expansion) in terms of standard chemical potential, activity, and inner (Galvani) potential:
$$
\bar{\mu}_i^{\alpha}=\mu_i^{\alpha}+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
=\mu_i^{0,\alpha}+RT\ln\!\big(a_i^{\alpha}\big)+z_iF\phi^{\alpha}
$$

For $\mathrm{ACh}^+$, $z=+1$. Substituting the expansion into the equilibrium condition gives the explicit “Nernst starting point”:

$$
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}\big)+F\phi^{W}
=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}\big)+F\phi^{NB}
$$

#### 7.3 Derivation Mode: interfacial potential difference (Nernst form for ion transfer)

Define the interfacial Galvani potential difference (organic relative to water) as  
$$
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}\equiv \phi^{NB}-\phi^{W}
$$

Then:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}\big)+F\phi^{W}
&=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}+RT\ln\!\big(a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}\big)+F\phi^{NB} \\
F(\phi^{NB}-\phi^{W})
&=
\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}
+RT\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}}\right) \\
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}
&=
\frac{\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}}{F}
+\frac{RT}{F}\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

It is common to express the standard-state term using a **standard Gibbs free energy of transfer**. Define
$$
\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{0,W\to NB}\equiv \mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}
$$
Then $\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,W}-\mu_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{0,NB}=-\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{0,W\to NB}$, and the result becomes
$$
\boxed{
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}
=-\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{0,W\to NB}}{F}
+\frac{RT}{F}\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{W}}{a_{\mathrm{ACh}^+}^{NB}}\right)
}
$$
or, more generally for charge number $z_i$,  
$$
\boxed{
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}
=-\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{0,W\to NB}}{z_iF}
+\frac{RT}{z_iF}\ln\!\left(\frac{a_{i}^{W}}{a_{i}^{NB}}\right).
}
$$

This is the ITIES analogue of the Nernst equation: the interfacial potential difference required for equilibrium depends on a standard transfer free energy (reflecting solvation preference between phases) and on the logarithm of the activity ratio across the interface.

Under that standard idealization, the solution-potential term cancels:

$$
2 F E=-R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S_1}}{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S_2}}\right)
$$


Divide by $2 F$ and rewrite the log ratio:

$$
E=\frac{R T}{2 F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S_2}}{a_{\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}}^{S_1}}\right)
$$


This is the Nernst-form terminal voltage for the zinc concentration cell. The standard potential $E^{\circ}$ cancels because both half-cells are the same redox couple; only the activity ratio drives the cell.




**3) Hydrogen concentration cell (keeps gas activity explicit; good for sign discipline).**  


> [!exercise]
> 
> $$
> \mathrm{Cu}\,|\,\mathrm{Pt}\,|\,\mathrm{H_2}(g_1)\,|\,\mathrm{H}^{+}(S_1)\,;\,;\,\mathrm{H}^{+}(S_2)\,|\,\mathrm{H_2}(g_2)\,|\,\mathrm{Pt}\,|\,\mathrm{Cu}'.
> $$  
> Assume all interfaces are at equilibrium. Derive the boxed expression for $E$ in terms of $a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S_1}$, $a_{\mathrm{H}^+}^{S_2}$, $a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g_1}$, $a_{\mathrm{H_2}}^{g_2}$, and $E^\circ$ (and then simplify $E^\circ$ if it cancels).


The hydrogen concentration cell differs from the zinc concentration cell only in what carries the chemicalstate information: here both $\mathrm{H}^{+}$in solution and $\mathrm{H}_2$ in the gas phase appear in the equilibrium condition. The strategy is the same as before: write a terminal-referenced equilibrium for the left side (against electrons in Cu ), write a terminal-referenced equilibrium for the right side (against electrons in $\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ ), then subtract to eliminate $\mu_e$ and isolate $E=\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}{ }^{\prime}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}$.

The complete cell is

$$
\mathrm{Cu}|\mathrm{Pt}| \mathrm{H}_2\left(g_1\right)\left|\mathrm{H}^{+}\left(S_1\right) ; ; \mathrm{H}^{+}\left(S_2\right)\right| \mathrm{H}_2\left(g_2\right)|\mathrm{Pt}| \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}, \quad E \equiv \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}} .
$$


### 1) Left side (compartment 1) written against electrons in the left copper terminal Cu

At the $\mathrm{Pt} \mid S_1$ interface, the redox equilibrium is

$$
2 \mathrm{H}^{+}\left(S_1\right)+2 e^{-}(\mathrm{Pt}) \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_2\left(g_1\right), \quad 2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1} .
$$


Electron equilibrium across the $\mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}$ junction gives

$$
\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}},
$$

so the left-side equilibrium can be written directly against electrons in the Cu terminal:

$$
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1} .
$$


Now expand using $\bar{\mu}_i=\mu_i^0+R T \ln a_i+z_i F \phi$, with $z_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}=+1, z_e=-1, z_{\mathrm{H}_2}=0$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1} \\
2\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)+F \phi^{S_1}\right)+2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right) & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\right) \\
2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)+2 F \phi^{S_1}+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}} & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$



### 2) Right side (compartment 2) written against electrons in the right copper terminal $\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ Similarly, at the $\mathrm{Pt} \mid S_2$ interface,

$$
2 \mathrm{H}^{+}\left(S_2\right)+2 e^{-}(\mathrm{Pt}) \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_2\left(g_2\right), \quad 2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2},
$$

and electron equilibrium across $\mathrm{Pt} \mid \mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}$ gives $\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Pt}}=\bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}$, so

$$
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}=\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2} .
$$


Expand:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}+2 \bar{\mu}_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} & =\bar{\mu}_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2} \\
2\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)+F \phi^{S_2}\right)+2\left(\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}\right) & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\right) \\
2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)+2 F \phi^{S_2}+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} & =\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$



### 3) Subtract to eliminate $\mu_e$, isolate $E$, and collect the log terms

Subtract $(R)$ from $(L)$. This subtraction is chosen because the copper terminals are identical, so $\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}= \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}{ }^{\prime}}$, and the electron chemical potentials cancel.

$$
\begin{aligned}
& (L)-(R): \\
& \left(2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)+2 F \phi^{S_1}+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right) \\
& \quad-\left(2 \mu_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{0, S}+2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)+2 F \phi^{S_2}+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right) \\
& \quad=\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\right)\right)-\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{0, g}+R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\right)\right) \\
\Longleftrightarrow & 2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)-2 R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)+2 F \phi^{S_1}-2 F \phi^{S_2}+2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}-2 \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}-2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}+2 F \phi^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}} \\
& =R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\right)-R T \ln \left(a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\right) \\
\Longleftrightarrow & \left.2 F\left(\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}-\phi^{\mathrm{Cu}}\right)=R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}}\right)-2 R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}}\right)-2 F\left(\phi^{S_1}-\phi^{S_2}\right) \quad \text { (use } \mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}}=\mu_e^{\mathrm{Cu}^{\prime}}\right) \\
\Longleftrightarrow & 2 F E=R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}}\right)+2 R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}}\right)-2 F\left(\phi^{S_1}-\phi^{S_2}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$


For the ideal "Nernst-form" concentration-cell result, the bulk-solution inner-potential difference is taken to contribute no additional unknown term (equivalently, the liquid-junction contribution is neglected in this thermodynamic derivation), so $\phi^{S_1}=\phi^{S_2}$. Under that standard idealization,

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 F E & =R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}}\right)+2 R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}}{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}}\right) \\
& =R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)^2}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)^2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$


Divide by $2 F$ :

$$
E=\frac{R T}{2 F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}\right)^2}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}\left(a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}\right)^2}\right)
$$

equivalently,

$$
E=\frac{R T}{2 F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_1}}{a_{\mathrm{H}_2}^{g_2}}\right)+\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_2}}{a_{\mathrm{H}^{+}}^{S_1}}\right) .
$$


Because both electrodes are the same $\mathrm{H}^{+} / \mathrm{H}_2$ couple, the standard potentials are identical and cancel, so $E^{\circ}$ for the cell is zero; the terminal voltage is driven only by the activity ratios.

## Practice set 2: ITIES ion-transfer problems with a boxed Nernst-type ($\Delta\phi$)




For each problem below, take the interfacial Galvani potential difference as  
$$
\Delta\phi_{W}^{NB}\equiv \phi^{NB}-\phi^{W},
$$  
assume equilibrium with respect to transfer, expand $\bar\mu$ fully, and express the final result as a boxed Nernst-type relation in terms of $\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}}^{0,W\to NB}$ (or equivalently a standard transfer potential) and an activity ratio.




**7) Monovalent cation transfer (same as ACh(^+), but do it generically).**  


> [!exercise]
> 
> $$
> C^{+}(W)\rightleftharpoons C^{+}(NB).
> $$  
> Derive a boxed expression for $\Delta\phi_W^{NB}$ in terms of $a_{C^+}^{W}$, $a_{C^+}^{NB}$, and $\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr},C^+}^{0,W\to NB}$. Make sure the sign convention matches your definition of $\Delta\phi_W^{NB}$.
> 


For a liquid|liquid ion-transfer equilibrium, the measurable interfacial driving force is the Galvani (inner) potential difference between the two phases. With nitrobenzene taken relative to water, define

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B} \equiv \phi^{N B}-\phi^W .
$$


At equilibrium with respect to transfer of $C^{+}$, the electrochemical potential of the ion must be equal in the two phases:

$$
\bar{\mu}_{C^{+}}^W=\bar{\mu}_{C^{+}}^{N B} .
$$


Expand each electrochemical potential using

$$
\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha=\mu_i^{0, \alpha}+R T \ln \left(a_i^\alpha\right)+z_i F \phi^\alpha, \quad z_{C^{+}}=+1, \quad \alpha \in\{W, N B\} .
$$


Substituting into the equilibrium condition gives the explicit starting point:

$$
\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}+R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^W\right)+F \phi^W=\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^{N B}\right)+F \phi^{N B}
$$


Now isolate the interfacial potential difference in Derivation Mode:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}+R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^W\right)+F \phi^W & =\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^{N B}\right)+F \phi^{N B} \\
F \phi^{N B}-F \phi^W & =\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}-\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^W\right)-R T \ln \left(a_{C^{+}}^{N B}\right) \\
F\left(\phi^{N B}-\phi^W\right) & =\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}-\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{C^{+}}^W}{a_{C^{+}}^{N B}}\right) \\
\Delta \phi_W^{N B} & =\frac{\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}-\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}}{F}+\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{C^{+}}^W}{a_{C^{+}}^{N B}}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$


Introduce the standard Gibbs free energy of transfer (water → nitrobenzene) as

$$
\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, C^{+}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B} \equiv \mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}-\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W} .
$$



Then $\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, W}-\mu_{C^{+}}^{0, N B}=-\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, C^{+}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}$, so the Nernst-type relation becomes

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=-\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, C^{+}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}}{F}+\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{C^{+}}^W}{a_{C^{+}}^{N B}}\right)
$$

which matches the sign convention $\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=\phi^{N B}-\phi^W$.



**8) Monovalent anion transfer (forces $z=-1$ all the way through).**  

> [!exercise]
> 
> $$
> A^{-}(W)\rightleftharpoons A^{-}(NB).
> $$  
> Derive the boxed expression for $\Delta\phi_W^{NB}$ in terms of $a_{A^-}^{W}$, $a_{A^-}^{NB}$, and $\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr},A^-}^{0,W\to NB}$. Do not “fix” signs at the end—carry $z=-1$ explicitly and simplify only at the last line.


Define the interfacial Galvani potential difference (nitrobenzene relative to water) as

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B} \equiv \phi^{N B}-\phi^W .
$$


At equilibrium with respect to transfer of $A^{-}$, the electrochemical potential of the ion must be equal in the two phases:

$$
\bar{\mu}_{A^{-}}^W=\bar{\mu}_{A^{-}}^{N B} .
$$


For a phase $\alpha \in\{W, N B\}$, expand the electrochemical potential as

$$
\bar{\mu}_i^\alpha=\mu_i^{0, \alpha}+R T \ln \left(a_i^\alpha\right)+z_i F \phi^\alpha, \quad z_{A^{-}}=-1 .
$$


Substituting into the equilibrium condition gives the explicit starting point:

$$
\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}+R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^W\right)+z F \phi^W=\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^{N B}\right)+z F \phi^{N B}, \quad(z=-1)
$$


Now isolate the interfacial potential difference in Derivation Mode while keeping $z$ explicit until the final line:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}+R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^W\right)+z F \phi^W & =\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^{N B}\right)+z F \phi^{N B} \\
z F \phi^{N B}-z F \phi^W & =\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^W\right)-R T \ln \left(a_{A^{-}}^{N B}\right) \\
z F\left(\phi^{N B}-\phi^W\right) & =\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^W}{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}\right) \\
z F \Delta \phi_W^{N B} & =\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}+R T \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^W}{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}\right) \\
\Delta \phi_W^{N B} & =\frac{\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}}{z F}+\frac{R T}{z F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^W}{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$


Introduce the standard Gibbs free energy of transfer (water → nitrobenzene) as

$$
\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, A^{-}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B} \equiv \mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}
$$


Then $\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, W}-\mu_{A^{-}}^{0, N B}=-\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, A^{-}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}$, so

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=-\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, A^{-}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}}{z F}+\frac{R T}{z F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^W}{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}\right) .
$$


Finally substitute $z=-1$ (only here) to obtain the boxed result:

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, A^{-}}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}}{F}-\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^W}{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}\right)
$$

equivalently,

$$
\Delta \phi_W^{N B}=\frac{\Delta G_{\mathrm{tr}, A-}^{0, W \rightarrow N B}}{F}+\frac{R T}{F} \ln \left(\frac{a_{A^{-}}^{N B}}{a_{A^{-}}^W}\right)
$$
