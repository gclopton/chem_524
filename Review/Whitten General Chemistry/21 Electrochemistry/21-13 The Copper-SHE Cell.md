# 21-13 The Copper-SHE Cell

Another voltaic cell consists of a SHE in one beaker and a strip of Cu metal immersed in $1 M$ copper(II) sulfate solution in another beaker. A wire and a salt bridge complete the circuit. For this cell, we observe the following (Figure 21-10).

1. The initial cell potential is 0.337 volt.
2. Gaseous hydrogen is consumed. The $\mathrm{H}^{+}$concentration increases in the solution of the SHE.
3. The mass of the copper electrode increases. The concentration of $\mathrm{Cu}^{2+}$ ions decreases in the solution around the copper electrode.

Thus, the following half-reactions and cell reaction occur.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/c942e4e5-a003-4e4b-91e8-7898f585fef6-17.jpg?height=180&width=1148&top_left_y=1774&top_left_x=596)

The SHE functions as the anode in this cell, and $\mathrm{Cu}^{2+}$ ions oxidize $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ to $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ions. The standard electrode potential of the copper half-cell is 0.337 volt as a cathode in the Cu-SHE cell.

Again, we can think of $E_{\text {oxidation }}^{0}$ in the two half-cells as "electron pressures."

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\mathrm{Cu} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cu}^{2+}+2 e^{-} & E_{\text {oxidation }}^{0}=-0.337 \mathrm{~V} \\
\mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{H}^{+}+2 e^{-} & E_{\text {oxidation }}^{0}=0.000 \mathrm{~V}
\end{array}
$$

Now we see that the hydrogen electrode has the higher electron pressure (more positive, less negative $\left.E_{\text {oxid }}^{0}\right)$.
