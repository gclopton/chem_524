# 21-5 The Electrolysis of Aqueous Sodium Sulfate

In the electrolysis of aqueous sodium sulfate using inert electrodes, we observe the following.

1. Gaseous $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ is produced at one electrode. The solution becomes basic around that electrode.
2. Gaseous $\mathrm{O}_{2}$ is produced at the other electrode. The solution becomes acidic around that electrode.

As in the previous example, water is reduced in preference to $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$at the cathode. Observation 2 suggests that water is also preferentially oxidized relative to the sulfate ion, $\mathrm{SO}_{4}{ }^{2-}$, at the anode (Figure 21-4).

$$
\begin{aligned}
2\left(2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+2 e^{-}\right. & \left.\longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}+2 \mathrm{OH}^{-}\right) \\
2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} & \text { (reduction, cathode) } \\
\hline 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{H}^{+}+4 e^{-}
\end{aligned} \text {(oxidation, anode) } \text { }
$$
